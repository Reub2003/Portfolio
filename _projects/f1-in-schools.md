## What is F1 in Schools?

F1 in Schools is an international STEM competition where student teams design, manufacture, and race miniature CO₂-powered Formula 1 cars. Teams are judged not just on race performance but on engineering design, portfolio, marketing, and verbal presentation — it's as close to a real engineering project as you can get at school level.

## The Cars

### 2017 Car — Learning the craft

The 2017 car was my first serious attempt at engineering design. Working through the fundamentals of aerodynamics and drag reduction for the first time, we placed **5th at the school competition**. It wasn't a winning result but it was where everything clicked — I got my first real exposure to CFD simulation and CNC manufacturing, and realised how much depth there was to explore.

![2017 car](/Portfolio/assets/images/f1-2017-car.jpg)

### 2018 Car — Winning design

Taking everything I learned in 2017, I redesigned the car from the ground up for 2018. The focus was on reducing drag through more refined aerodynamic shaping and tighter tolerances in manufacturing. The result: **1st place at school**, and the team went on to win the **SEA Regional Finals**.

![2018 car](/Portfolio/assets/images/f1-2018-car.jpg)

## CFD & Manufacturing

This was genuinely my first time using CFD software in a meaningful way — running simulations, interpreting results, and feeding them back into the design. Alongside that, I was involved in the physical manufacturing of the car, which taught me how much a design can change between CAD and the finished part.

The combination of simulation and hands-on fabrication was what made this project formative. It wasn't just about getting a good result — it was the process of iterating between digital and physical that stuck with me.

![CFD simulation](/Portfolio/assets/images/f1-cfd.jpg)

## Abu Dhabi 2019

After the SEA Finals win, the team went on to compete in Abu Dhabi in 2019. The highlight was visiting the Mercedes AMG F1 camp, where the team met **Toto Wolff and Lewis Hamilton** — a pretty surreal experience for a group of students who'd spent years obsessing over aerodynamics.

![Abu Dhabi / Mercedes camp](/Portfolio/assets/images/f1-abu-dhabi.jpg)

## Why it mattered

F1 in Schools was the first time I genuinely lost track of time working on an engineering problem. The combination of aerodynamics, manufacturing constraints, and competition pressure made it feel real in a way that school classes didn't. It's where I decided I wanted to pursue aerospace and aerodynamics as a career.
