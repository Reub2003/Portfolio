## Overview

Two separate simulation projects from university, both pushing into territory I first touched back in F1 in Schools — but at a significantly higher level of rigour.

---

## Project 1 — CFD: Battery Cooling Chamber

### The problem

Batteries generate heat under load, and managing that heat is one of the central engineering challenges in electric vehicles and energy storage systems. The brief was to design a **cooling chamber for a battery pack** and validate the design through CFD simulation.

### The approach

The design process was iterative — starting with a geometry, running the simulation, interpreting the thermal and flow results, and refining. Key considerations were:

- Uniform temperature distribution across the battery cells
- Minimising pressure drop through the cooling channels
- Practical geometry that could realistically be manufactured

![CFD cooling chamber simulation](/Portfolio/assets/images/cfd-cooling-result.jpg)

### What I learned

CFD at university is a different discipline from the rough simulations I ran in F1 in Schools. Mesh quality, boundary condition setup, solver convergence — the details matter a lot more when you're trying to produce results you can stand behind. This project was where I started treating simulation as an engineering tool rather than just a visualisation.

## Project 2 — FEM: Brake Disc Thermal-Structural Analysis

### The problem

Brake discs experience extreme thermal loads during braking events, and those thermal loads produce structural stresses that can lead to fatigue and failure. The project involved modelling a brake disc under realistic loading conditions and analysing how heat input translated into structural stress.

### The approach

This was a **coupled thermal-structural analysis** — first solving the thermal problem (heat generated by braking, conduction through the disc), then using those temperature results as inputs to the structural model to find the resulting stress distribution.

![FEM brake disc](/Portfolio/assets/images/fem-brake-disc.jpg)

![Stress distribution](/Portfolio/assets/images/fem-stress-result.jpg)

### What I learned

Coupled multi-physics analysis is where FEM gets genuinely interesting — and genuinely difficult. Getting the two models to talk to each other correctly, and making sure the results were physically reasonable, required understanding both the thermal and structural sides of the problem rather than treating them in isolation.
