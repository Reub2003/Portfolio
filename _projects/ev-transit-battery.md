## The Brief

As part of DESN1000 at UNSW, our team had to design and build a small electric vehicle capable of moving under its own power. The twist: we couldn't just use off-the-shelf batteries. We had to **build our own battery cells** from scratch — assembling them from raw electrodes, anodes, and electrolyte.

## Building the Battery

This was genuinely outside my comfort zone. My background coming in was mechanical and aerodynamic — electrochemistry was new territory. Constructing cells from individual components meant understanding how charge actually moves through a system, getting the chemistry right, and then making something that would reliably deliver enough voltage and current to drive a motor.

![Battery assembly](/Portfolio/assets/images/ev-battery-assembly.jpg)

There was a lot of trial and error. Getting the cell chemistry to behave consistently was harder than expected, and connecting multiple cells into a pack that didn't fail under load took several iterations.

## The Vehicle

Once the battery pack was working, it had to actually drive something. The vehicle design had to be lightweight enough that our homemade cells could move it, which added another layer of constraint to the whole system.

![Completed vehicle](/Portfolio/assets/images/ev-vehicle.jpg)

## What I took from it

Honestly, this project humbled me. Coming from a background in mechanical design and CFD, I was used to having some intuition for how a system would behave. With electrochemistry, I had to start from scratch — reading, testing, and accepting that I didn't know what I was doing yet.

That experience of being genuinely out of your depth and working through it anyway is probably one of the more useful things a first-year engineering course can teach you.
